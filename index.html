From 2001 to 2016 I was Assistant, and then Associate, Professor of Chemistry at the University of Minnesota. My research group studied hybrid materials made from organic polymers and inorganic nanoparticles. We aimed to develop biomedical materials with therapeutic or diagnostic value, but we also invented new materials for their own sake. Why Switch?

Frankly, I wasn't a great professor. Professors at research-intensive institutions like the University of Minnesota succeed on the strength of their research. But I struggled to build a research program that was anywhere as innovative or impactful as the ones I had worked in as a graduate student or postdoc. After the rush of earning tenure, I came to hate writing grant applications, not just because professors have to do it so much, but also because I lacked faith in the ideas I wrote about. I was also a pretty undistingished teacher. I got better with time and training, but my giant undergraduate organic chemistry courses came to feel more like taskwork than education.

In 2012, I spoke with my department head about winding down my research group, and potentially leaving my job. He drew up a "memorandum of understanding" that modified my annual post-tenure review while I shrank my lab. In 2014, one of my last remaining students was involved in a lab accident that sent him to the hospital (with injuries he thankfully recovered from fully). It wasn't my fault, but I certainly felt responsible, and it cemented my decision to leave.

I have always enjoyed working with computers. I was my group's network admin as a postdoc and as a professor, and I got to do some web and instrument automation programming in both of those positions as well. And, of course, I am amazed by programming's breathtaking potential. In my last year at the university, I decided to do some self-study, and then to apply to Prime Academy in Minneapolis. I was already enrolled at Prime when I left my position at the U of M.
